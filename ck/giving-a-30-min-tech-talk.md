# Giving a 30-Minute Tech Talk

**Compiled from:** Sebastian Witowski (switowski.com), Emily Riederer (emilyriederer.com), Michael Ernst / University of Washington (April 2026)

---

## Part 1 — How to Make a Great Conference Talk (Sebastian Witowski)

*Source: switowski.com/blog/how-to-make-a-great-conference-talk*

### Why Bother Speaking?

- Free conference tickets
- Recognition and professional credibility
- Networking and new connections
- Future speaking invitations
- Doors to new projects

> "The more presentations you give, the more famous you get."

### CFP (Call for Proposals) Strategy

- Make proposals **easy to scan** — use bullet points, not dense paragraphs
- Clearly explain **what the audience will take away**
- Include links to previous presentations
- Use catchy but honest titles — avoid clickbait
- Submit multiple proposals to increase acceptance chances

### Talk Structure

Classic three-part format:
1. **Introduction** — hook, context, problem
2. **Main content** — your solution / insights
3. **Summary** — repeat your takeaways

> "Repeat takeaways multiple times" — audiences need repetition to retain.

### 30-Minute Timing Checkpoints

During rehearsal, note your position at:
- **10-minute mark** — should be finishing introduction / entering main content
- **20-minute mark** — should be through the bulk of the content
- **25-minute mark** — should be in final summary, with 5 min buffer for Q&A

These checkpoints help you calibrate pace and identify where to cut.

### Slides Best Practices

- No walls of text
- Large fonts (readable from the back of the room)
- Reveal bullet points progressively — don't show everything at once
- Minimal design — slides support the talk, they don't replace it
- **Slides are a support tool, not a script**

### Rehearsal & Delivery

- Rehearse thoroughly — record yourself for feedback
- Perfect the **opening** and **closing** above all else
- On stage: connect with the audience, vary your pace
- Slow down for key points; speed up during context-setting

### Managing Q&A

- "I don't know" is acceptable — say it confidently
- Redirect detailed rabbit holes to post-talk discussions
- Have a backup plan if you want to skip Q&A

## Part 2 — Notes on Preparing a Tech Talk (Emily Riederer)

*Source: emilyriederer.netlify.app/post/writing-a-tech-talk*

### The 6-Step Framework

#### Step 1: Establish Your Call to Action

Every talk needs a clear purpose. The "action" doesn't require the audience to *do* something — it can be:
- A shift in how they think about a problem
- Making them wonder about something new
- Inspiring them to experiment

> Identify **one compelling takeaway** that audiences remember the next morning.

#### Step 2: Shape the Story (Before Slides)

Build your outline first using **topic sentences** that tell your story logically.

> **Do NOT jump to slide design first.** This locks your thinking and wastes effort if the narrative changes.

Each topic sentence gets no more than 2–3 supporting subpoints (adjust for time slot).

#### Step 3: Test the Outline

Practice your outline aloud. Check it is:
- Appropriate length
- Motivated by your call to action
- Coherent and natural-sounding
- Interesting in your authentic voice

#### Step 4: Create Slides

Slides illustrate the story — they don't duplicate it.
- Let narrative determine slide quantity
- Use visuals instead of dense text
- Include branding on title slides (social handles, etc.)

#### Step 5: Practice Thoroughly

1. Practice alone first — refine phrasing and delivery
2. Record yourself — identify tone and pacing issues
3. Present to a small test audience for honest feedback
4. Practice until the story flows without mental effort

#### Step 6: Don't Overthink

Her framework is guidance, not a rigid process. Different speakers develop talks differently. Goal: story development before visual design.

### Key Mindset: Authenticity Over Performance

> "You'll have enough on your mind when presenting without learning how to act."

## Part 3 — How to Give a Technical Presentation (Michael Ernst, UW)

*Source: homes.cs.washington.edu/~mernst/advice/giving-talk.html*

### The Three Things You Must Convince Your Audience Of

1. **The problem is worthwhile** — why should they care?
2. **The problem is genuinely difficult** — why hasn't it been solved?
3. **You've solved it** — your contribution

Missing any one element significantly reduces talk effectiveness.

### The Pacing Rule

> **About one slide per minute is a good pace** for presentations with minimal animations.

For a 30-minute talk: aim for ~25–28 slides (leave buffer for questions).

### Content Strategy

**Elide everything that does not support your key points.**

- Don't compress an entire technical paper into slides
- Create an *advertisement* for your work — present key ideas and compelling results
- Prioritize intuitions and conceptual explanations over technical minutiae
- A lighter treatment that leaves audiences wanting more beats rushing through too much

### Slide Design Rules

**Titles:**
- Use descriptive titles that capture each slide's specific contribution
- Avoid repetitive titles that don't clarify progression

**Text:**
- Bullet points: telegraphic — "3 lines of text for a bullet point is always too much"
- Don't read slides verbatim — bores audiences and creates pacing mismatch
- Large, legible fonts readable from the back of the room
- Sans-serif fonts for screen readability

**Visuals:**
- Use diagrams, architecture drawings, system visualizations
- Color + callouts (arrows, highlighting) for emphasis
- ~5% of American males are color-blind — supplement color with other emphasis techniques
- When slides build on previous ones: all common elements must stay pixel-for-pixel in the same position

### Opening & Closing

**Opening:**
- Never begin with an outline slide
- Start with motivation and a concrete example demonstrating why the problem matters
- First slide should illustrate the problem you're solving

**Closing:**
- End with a contributions/conclusions slide reinforcing your main message
- Leave this slide up during Q&A — it's your final impression
- Do NOT end with "Thank You," "Questions," or contact info

### Outline Slides (for 30+ min talks)

Use sparingly to reorient audiences — indicate current section through color, formatting, or arrows.

### Delivery

- Stand and face the audience — seated talks lack energy
- Eye contact — gauge comprehension and interest
- Never face the screen or look at your laptop
- Don't stand in front of slides
- Avoid pacing — signals nervousness

**Pointing at slides:**
- Physically touch the screen or come within an inch
- Laser pointers shake when presenters are nervous
- Using your arm creates more dynamic energy

**Nervousness:**
- Practice until verbal tics ("um") disappear
- If flustered: pause, drink water — natural regrouping
- You can skip material without the audience noticing

### Q&A

- **Repeat questions back** before answering — confirms understanding, provides thinking time, repeats inaudible questions for the room
- "I don't know" is far better than fabricating an answer
- Dodging questions damages credibility more than admitting knowledge gaps

### Practice Protocol

1. Deliver at least one **full practice talk aloud** before the real thing
2. Number slides for easy reference during feedback
3. Keep practice audiences under 10 people for candid feedback
4. Videotape yourself — see what others see
5. For high-stakes talks: multiple practice runs

## Summary: 30-Minute Talk Playbook

| Phase | Time | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| **Hook / problem** | 0–3 min | Why should they care? Make it painful or surprising |
| **Context / landscape** | 3–8 min | What exists today, what's missing |
| **Core content** | 8–22 min | Your solution / techniques / demos |
| **Takeaways** | 22–27 min | Repeat key points, what to do next |
| **Q&A buffer** | 27–30 min | Questions, wrap-up |

### The Checklist Before You Walk On Stage

- [ ] One clear takeaway you can say in one sentence
- [ ] Story built before slides were created
- [ ] Opening memorized and polished
- [ ] Closing slide ready (not "Thank You")
- [ ] Practiced aloud at least 3 times
- [ ] Timing checkpoints hit at 10, 20, 25 minutes
- [ ] ~1 slide per minute pacing
- [ ] No walls of text on any slide
